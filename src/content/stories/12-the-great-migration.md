Gunner knew something was wrong the moment the boxes appeared.

Boxes meant the vet. Gunner had established this connection through years of careful observation: when Mom got out the crate, they went to the vet. When Dad loaded things into the truck, they went somewhere. Going somewhere sometimes meant the vet. Therefore, boxes equaled vet. It was airtight logic, as far as Gunner's logic went.

He retreated under the kitchen table and watched the chaos with the mournful eyes of a dog who had already accepted his fate.

Tiger, by contrast, knew exactly what was happening. He'd known for days. He'd read the signs the way a cat reads everything — not through the obvious (the boxes, the tape, the moving truck in the driveway) but through the subtle. The conversations between Mom and Dad after the boys went to bed, spoken in low voices that Tiger could hear from two rooms away. The way Mom looked at the garden when she walked through it, lingering at each bed, touching the plants. The way Dad stood on the porch at sunset, staring at the fifteen acres with the face of a man memorizing something he couldn't take with him.

They were leaving.

Tiger had opinions about this.

---

Moving day was a Saturday in March. The East Texas morning was mild and damp, with fog sitting in the low places like cotton batting. The U-Haul was parked in the driveway, its mouth open, waiting to swallow their lives.

The boys moved through the house in different states. The oldest carried boxes with the steady, silent efficiency of a kid who understood that work was the best way to handle feelings you didn't know what to do with. He didn't complain. He didn't slow down. He just carried.

The middle one kept disappearing. Mom would find him in his room, or in the barn, or standing in the garden looking at something only he could see. He was the dreamer, and this place had been his dreamscape — fifteen acres of forests and creeks and hiding spots that he'd turned into kingdoms. He was twelve, old enough to understand they were leaving, young enough for it to hurt in a way he couldn't articulate.

The youngest was handling it by helping with maximum enthusiasm. He carried things — some of them the wrong things, most of them to the wrong places — with an energy that suggested he either didn't fully grasp the permanence of what was happening or had processed it and decided that speed was the cure.

Mom was everywhere. She taped boxes, directed traffic, answered forty-seven questions from three boys, made lunch that nobody sat down to eat, and maintained the particular kind of calm that mothers maintain when everything is changing and everyone is watching to see if she's okay.

She was okay. She was always okay. She had to be.

Dad loaded the heavy things and did the math — what fit where, how much weight in the truck, what went in the car, what went in the trailer. He was good at logistics. He was a Cloud Architect; his whole career was building structures in the air. But this was different. This was dismantling a structure on the ground — a real one, a physical one, made of wood and dirt and four years of a family growing into a place.

"We'll find another place," he told Mom.

"I know," she said.

"It'll have land."

"I know."

"And chickens."

She smiled. It didn't reach her eyes, but it reached her mouth, and that was enough for now.

---

Gunner, still under the table, watched the furniture leave the house one piece at a time. The couch went. The bookshelf went. The boys' bunk beds went, disassembled into poles and planks that looked nothing like beds anymore.

When Dad started dismantling Gunner's corner — the spot by the heater where his bed lived, where Tiger slept on top of him every night, where the world was warm and safe and smelled like home — Gunner whined.

Not a loud whine. A small one. The kind dogs make when they're confused and there's nobody to explain things to them, because dogs can't be told "we're moving to be closer to Grandpa, who is sick, and we need to be near the hospital, and I'm sorry, buddy, I know this was your home."

Dogs just know that things are changing and they don't know why.

Tiger jumped off the windowsill and walked to Gunner under the table. He didn't rub against him. Didn't purr. Just sat beside him, close enough to touch, and faced the room with that steady, unblinking calm that was Tiger's version of saying: *I'm here. Whatever this is, I'm here.*

Gunner pressed his side against Tiger's side.

They sat like that while the house emptied around them.

---

The animals went last.

Tiger went into his crate with the dignified resignation of royalty entering a carriage that was beneath his station. The crate was lined with the fleece blanket he'd claimed as his own — the one Mom had bought for the couch that Tiger had immediately annexed — and it smelled like home. Tiger circled twice, lay down, and closed his eyes.

He'd been through worse. He was a barn cat's son, born on these fifteen acres when it was still wild to him. He'd survived his siblings disappearing, his mother leaving, the coyotes on cold nights. A move was just geography. Home wasn't a place. Home was a warm surface that breathed.

He could see Gunner's crate across the back seat, and beyond it, through the car window, the house — small, white, already looking empty, already looking like someone else's.

Gunner's crate was larger and contained a dog who did not want to be in it. He lay on his side, taking up every inch, his nose pressed against the door, his eyes tracking every movement of Dad and the boys as they made their last trips.

"It's okay, Gunner," the youngest said, pressing his face against the crate door. "We're coming back."

They weren't coming back. Everyone knew this except possibly the youngest and definitely Gunner. But the youngest said it with such conviction that even Tiger felt something shift — not in his understanding, but in his chest. The place where feelings would go if Tiger admitted to having them, which he did not.

---

The oldest boy was the last one inside the house.

He'd gone back in to do a final sweep — checking closets, looking under beds, making sure nothing was left behind. He was thirteen, and this was the first place he'd ever had his own room, his own workbench in the barn, his own space in the world. He'd built things here. Learned things here. Grown from a kid into whatever comes after kid.

He stood in the empty living room for a moment. The walls had lighter rectangles where pictures had hung. The floor had dents where furniture had sat. The heater clicked on, warming a room that nobody would sit in tonight.

He closed the door behind him.

---

The caravan pulled out at 2 PM: the U-Haul first, with Dad and the oldest; the car behind it, with Mom, the two younger boys, and the animals. The trailer came last, hitched to the car, packed with the things that didn't fit anywhere else.

The fifteen acres unfolded in the rearview mirror — the fence, the pasture (empty now, the cows sold the week before), the barn, the pond, the tree line where Tiger had sat on fence posts watching the world. It got smaller. Then it was just a shape. Then it was gone.

Mom didn't look back. She was driving, and looking forward was her job, the way it was always her job.

The middle boy looked back. He watched until the last piece of fence disappeared behind the curve of the road, and then he turned around and put his hand on Tiger's crate. Tiger pressed his nose against the boy's fingers through the wire door.

The youngest fell asleep before they reached the highway, because that's what he did — ran at full speed until the switch flipped, and then he was out. He slept with one hand on Gunner's crate.

In the car, Tiger could hear Gunner breathing. Slow, deep breaths. The dog had calmed down, the way dogs eventually do — not because they understand, but because the car was moving and the family was together and the vibration of the road was steady and familiar.

Tiger purred. Very quietly. Not for Gunner, not for the boys, not for anyone. Just because purring was what Tiger did when the world was uncertain and the only solid thing was the sound he could make in his own chest.

The U-Haul's brake lights flashed ahead. Mom followed. The East Texas pines lined the highway like a farewell formation, tall and straight and green.

Somewhere ahead: a rented house near Waco, a smaller yard, an uncertain future.

Somewhere behind: fifteen acres of everything they'd been.

In between: a family in two vehicles, two boys and two animals in a car, moving forward because that's the only direction that works, even when every other direction is the one you want.
