It started with the butter.

Mom had set a stick of butter on the porch railing to soften — she was baking, and the kitchen was already too warm, and the porch was cool enough to do the job without melting it into a puddle. She'd been gone three minutes. Maybe four.

When she came back, the butter was gone. Not the dish. Just the butter. The ceramic dish sat there, licked clean, catching the afternoon sun like a tiny empty swimming pool.

"GUNNER."

Gunner, who was asleep on the porch approximately four feet from the scene of the crime, lifted his head with the expression of a dog who had no idea what was happening but was fairly certain he was in trouble.

"Did you eat my butter?"

Gunner's tail wagged cautiously. This was a trap. He could feel it. When humans asked questions in that tone, the correct answer was always to look as innocent as possible and wag slowly. He employed this strategy now.

Mom looked at Gunner. Gunner looked at Mom. There was no butter on his face. No evidence on his paws. His breath, while never pleasant, did not smell specifically of butter.

"Hmm," Mom said, and went inside for more butter.

Tiger, watching from the windowsill, noted the incident and filed it away.

---

The next day, it was the cornbread.

A pan of cornbread, cooling on the porch table, covered with a towel. The boys were doing their schoolwork inside. Mom was teaching a math lesson that involved fractions and patience in equal, dwindling quantities. Dad was on a call in his office, explaining cloud architecture to someone who did not want it explained but needed it explained, which was basically his entire career.

When Mom checked the porch twenty minutes later, the towel was on the ground, the pan was empty, and the cornbread had been removed with a precision that suggested either fingers or very dexterous paws.

"GUNNER!"

Gunner was in the living room. Asleep. He'd been asleep for an hour, as verified by the middle boy, who reported that Gunner had been snoring "so loud I couldn't focus on long division."

Mom looked at the empty pan. She looked at Tiger, who sat on the porch railing with an air of aristocratic innocence.

"Tiger?"

Tiger blinked slowly. The slow blink was cat language for many things — affection, contempt, and "I have no idea what you're talking about" were all in the range. Tiger meant the third one. Probably.

But Tiger was also thinking. Because Tiger had noticed something Mom hadn't. Small tracks in the dust near the porch steps. Five-toed. Not cat. Not dog.

Tiger knew exactly what was happening.

---

Over the next week, the Porch Bandit struck four more times:

A plate of biscuits — gone.
Three apples from a bowl — vanished.
A bag of dog treats that Dad had left on the railing — obliterated.
Half a sandwich that the youngest had abandoned mid-bite to go chase something (a bug, a bird, his own shadow — impossible to know) — disappeared.

Mom was bewildered. Dad was amused. The boys had developed a theory involving a neighbor's dog, a family of bears, and — from the middle boy — a ghost with an appetite.

Gunner was blamed for approximately sixty percent of the incidents, despite being asleep for most of them. This was unfair, but also understandable, because Gunner's relationship with unattended food was well-documented and historically disastrous.

Tiger decided it was time to act.

---

The first trap was simple.

Tiger positioned a bowl of cat food — his own dinner, sacrificed for the greater good — on the porch steps at dusk. He then stationed himself behind the rain barrel, approximately four feet away, in a crouch so low and so still that he was effectively invisible.

He waited.

The Virginia dusk deepened. Crickets started their evening shift. A whippoorwill called from the tree line. The shadows stretched across the yard like long fingers.

Twenty minutes passed.

Tiger was patient. Tiger was discipline. Tiger was —

A clicking of nails on the porch boards.

Tiger's ears rotated.

Gunner appeared from inside the house, nose working the air, following the scent of cat food the way a guided missile follows a heat signature. He walked directly to the bowl, looked at it, looked around to see if anyone was watching (they were — Tiger was watching with growing horror), and ate the entire contents in three bites.

Then he licked the bowl. Then he licked the porch around the bowl. Then he licked the air near where the bowl had been, just in case.

Tiger emerged from behind the rain barrel with the expression of a general whose own troops had just accidentally shelled his command post.

Gunner saw Tiger, wagged, and went back inside.

---

The second trap was more sophisticated.

Tiger recruited the middle boy, who was always up for an operation that involved stealth, planning, and staying up past bedtime. Together — through a communication system that involved Tiger staring pointedly at things and the boy interpreting those stares with surprising accuracy — they set up a perimeter.

The bait this time was a piece of chicken (from dinner, smuggled out by the boy under his napkin, which Mom noticed but chose not to address because she was tired). It was placed on a paper plate on the porch railing, directly under the motion-sensor light that Dad had installed after the third incident.

The middle boy hid behind the porch swing with a flashlight. Tiger took his position on top of the woodpile, eyes on the bait. Gunner was inside, barricaded in the living room by the oldest boy, who had been given the critical mission of keeping the dog occupied. He did this by sitting on the floor and letting Gunner lie on his lap, which was Gunner's idea of a perfect evening.

They waited.

Darkness fell. The motion light stayed off. The chicken sat on the railing, its smell drifting into the night air.

Forty minutes passed.

The middle boy's flashlight flickered. His breathing slowed. His head nodded forward, then jerked back.

Tiger's ears twitched. Not at the boy. At the tree line.

Movement. Low. Slow. Purposeful.

A shape emerged from the shadows at the edge of the yard. It moved across the grass with a rolling, waddle-like gait that was immediately recognizable to Tiger, who had been expecting this exact creature since the first butter incident.

Raccoon.

It was a big one — probably twenty pounds, with a dark mask across its face and hands — actual hands, with fingers — that moved with the casual dexterity of a creature that had been opening latches and solving problems since before dogs were invented.

The raccoon paused at the bottom of the porch steps. Its dark eyes scanned the area. Its nose twitched. It looked at the chicken on the railing the way Tiger looked at a freshly opened can of tuna — with assessment, not desperation.

It climbed the steps.

The motion light clicked on.

The raccoon froze, caught in the floodlight, hands on the second step, eyes wide. It looked, for one perfect moment, like a burglar in a spotlight.

"I SEE IT!" the middle boy shouted, flashlight swinging wildly.

The raccoon bolted. Not back into the yard — up. It went up the porch column like it was nothing, across the overhang, and onto the roof in approximately one and a half seconds.

Tiger launched from the woodpile, crossed the porch in three bounds, and made it to the base of the column in time to see a ringed tail disappearing over the roofline.

The commotion woke Gunner, who broke free from the oldest boy's restraining hug and burst through the front door.

"BOOF BOOF BOOF—"

He looked left. He looked right. He looked at the middle boy, who was pointing at the roof. He looked at Tiger, who was staring at the roof.

Gunner looked at the roof.

The raccoon looked down at all of them from the edge of the gutter, its face framed between its hands, expression unreadable but probably amused.

Gunner barked at the roof for eleven minutes straight.

The raccoon waited him out, then climbed down the back side of the house and disappeared into the woods with what Tiger was certain was a swagger.

The chicken, somehow, was still on the railing. Gunner ate it.

---

The third trap finally worked.

Tiger didn't bother with bait this time. He'd realized the fundamental flaw in his approach: bait attracted two things — the bandit and Gunner. The solution was to eliminate the bait and focus on territory.

So Tiger did what cats have done since the Egyptians — he simply sat on the porch. All night. Every night. Visible, alert, a small striped sentinel radiating the quiet, unblinking authority of a creature who has never once in his life backed down from a staring contest.

The first night, the raccoon came to the edge of the tree line, saw Tiger, and stopped. They stared at each other across the yard for a full three minutes. The raccoon assessed. Tiger assessed. Two predators, calculating.

The raccoon left.

The second night, it came closer. Made it to the bottom of the porch steps. Tiger didn't move. Didn't hiss. Just sat there, a loaf of focused intent, golden eyes catching the moonlight.

The raccoon left again.

The third night, it didn't come at all.

Tiger sat on the porch until dawn anyway, because a point made once is a suggestion. A point made consistently is a law.

---

"I think something was getting into the food on the porch," Dad said over breakfast a few days later, pouring coffee. "But it seems to have stopped."

"It was a raccoon," the middle boy said. "Tiger and I saw it."

"When were you and Tiger outside at night?" Mom asked, in a tone that suggested this question was not really a question.

"Hypothetically," the middle boy said.

Mom looked at the middle boy. The middle boy looked at his cereal. Tiger, on the kitchen counter, licked a paw.

Under the table, Gunner waited for gravity to do its work on someone's breakfast. It always did. Usually the youngest — he gestured too much when he talked, and things flew off his spoon with predictable frequency.

On cue, a piece of scrambled egg hit the floor.

Gunner inhaled it before it finished bouncing.

Tiger watched from the counter, made a note of the youngest boy's dropping pattern, and adjusted his positioning for next time.

Outside, at the treeline, a fat raccoon sat on a stump, watching the house. It could wait. It had all winter. And there was always the neighbors' trash cans.

But this porch — this particular porch — belonged to the cat now.

The raccoon understood that.
